I read all of it — the verification doc, the round-2 spectra and model fits, the round-3 head taxonomy, positional-horizon and SConv summaries, and all six figures. First, the trust check, then the plain-language story.

**Is the data trustworthy?** Yes, unusually so. The dump was re-fetched independently and matched byte-for-byte; the reconstruction of the bias was checked against the official HuggingFace implementation and agreed to *exactly zero* error on every tested layer; and the audit caught and quarantined three real bugs in the earlier fits (the raw "sigma" column is meaningless, the sinusoid model was over-parameterized, and the first few distances need separate treatment). What survives the audit is what I'll describe.

## The plain-language findings

**1. The model was free to invent any sense of distance — and it chose "closer is better," almost everywhere.**
Remember the setup: Inkling isn't given a hard-wired distance rule; it learns one from scratch, and the math says its learned rule could have been any mix of three flavors — rotate (periodic, oscillating), stretch (smooth decay/growth), or slide. When you actually fit the learned curves and let statistics pick the best-fitting shape, the dominant pattern in 57 of 66 layers is a smooth decay — attention preference fading with distance. The nine apparent exceptions turn out to be fakes: "waves" with a wavelength longer than the whole window, i.e., not waves at all. **There is no genuine oscillation anywhere in the dominant signal.** Given a blank sheet, the model essentially re-invented a fancier version of the simplest known scheme (a recency penalty), plus one sharp special case: a spike of attention on the *immediately previous* word or two, which is its own separate little mechanism.

**2. The heads did specialize — but by depth, not by flavor.**
Both of your elicitation runs had Inkling predict, blind and then fact-fed, the same thing: "crack open my weights and my heads will have diversified — some rotating, some stretching, some sliding." That prediction is **falsified**, and in an interesting way. Within any single layer, the 64 heads are strikingly *uniform* — the taxonomy shows whole layers of one class. The variety lives across depth instead, in three clean phases: early layers (roughly 0–13) where the bias actually *rises* with distance — the young layers push attention outward, away from the immediate neighborhood; a messy transition band (roughly 14–29); and then the entire back half of the network (30–65) locked into decay. The global long-range layers are decay-shaped from the very start. So the model organized its sense of distance like a pipeline — "look outward early, focus locally late" — rather than as a committee of differently-flavored heads.

**3. The "free" mechanism collapsed to nearly one dimension.**
Each layer's positional feature has 16 dimensions available. The effective number actually used starts around 2.5–3 in early layers and falls to about 1.1–1.4 in the deep layers. Inkling's own argument for why it *couldn't* be pinned to one flavor was "my projection is high-rank and unconstrained." Architecturally true — but training pinned it anyway. The freedom exists in the blueprint; the trained model barely uses it. That's the cleanest single sentence the data gives you: **the architecture permits everything; training chose almost nothing.**

**4. The seam is real, and it's a cliff.**
Your whole two-experiment arc centered on the prediction that splitting position-handling into pieces leaves an unremovable seam. The weights show one, in a place nobody guessed: the learned bias is defined only out to a fixed distance (512 for local layers, 1024 for global) and is hard-masked to **exactly zero** beyond it. And the curves do *not* politely taper to zero as they approach that edge — the typical layer is still at ~83% of full strength at the boundary, and the worst layers are five to six times *louder* at the edge than in the middle. So a token at distance 1023 gets a strong learned positional signal and a token at distance 1025 gets none at all. A cliff, not a ramp.

**5. Which leads to the headline: the 1M-token model's learned sense of distance ends at 1,024.**
Past distance ~1024, Inkling carries no learned positional information whatsoever — just the causal mask and a small logarithmic volume knob (verified to match the official formula, ~1.21× at a million tokens, which actually *amplifies* the cliff). Over 99.9% of its advertised context window, the model is navigating effectively position-blind, relying on content alone to know what's where. That's not necessarily a flaw — "no positional encoding at long range" is a known viable regime — but it's a very different animal from what the marketing implies, and it's the kind of thing only a weights dump reveals.

**6. The little local convolution is barely a mixer.**
The "short conv" turns out to be tiny — it reads only the current token plus the previous three. And two of its three copies are close to doing nothing: the attention-path and value-path kernels put ~96–99% of their energy on the current token. Only the key-path kernel does real local blending, and even that is concentrated in the earliest layers. So the "local shift" module your framework flagged as the pulled-out N-piece exists, but it's a whisper, not a workhorse — most of the local work happens elsewhere.

## What it means, honestly

The elicitation experiments told you what the architecture *permits*; the weights tell you what training *selected*. They diverge, and the divergence is the finding. Inkling reasoned correctly that nothing constrains its distance-sense to one geometric flavor — and then the actual optimization collapsed onto one flavor (monotone decay/growth) plus a previous-token reflex, organized by depth instead of by head, with a hard positional horizon at 1k tokens and an un-smoothed cliff at the seam. Its self-prediction was a fair test, fairly run, and it failed — which is more informative than if it had passed, because it says the pressure toward recency-shaped attention comes from *training on language*, not from the encoding scheme. Take away RoPE's compass and the model builds its own compass that points the same direction.

Two honest caveats before this goes anywhere load-bearing: this is weights-only — none of it yet shows these curves are *load-bearing* in behavior (your usual functional-use gate still applies; the ablation/intervention step is untouched); and the "rising vs. decay vs. other" labels come from threshold rules, so the three-phase depth story is robust but the exact phase boundaries shouldn't be quoted as sharp numbers. The cliff and the 1024 horizon, by contrast, are exact facts of the implementation — masked to zero in the official code, verified to the last decimal.
